RNA integrity

Posted on 2018-04-01

28S/18S

https://www.thermofisher.com/cn/zh/home/references/ambion-tech-support/rna-isolation/tech-notes/assessing-rna-quality.html

reason to use this value

28S and 18S are easy degraded, so we can estimate the integrity of RNA sample based on their presence

things to notice

  • in mammal, 28S/18S = 2.7:1 (usually use 2:1), but not sure in other species
  • when 28S/18S = 2:1, the RNA samples quality are good; but when 28S/18S < 2:1, the quality may not be bad

RIN

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_integrity_number

Its extact algorithm is proprietary

Ideal eletropherogram:

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_integrity_number#/media/File:Sample_RNA_electropherogram.png

Value ranges from 1 to 10

Points to indicate a good RNA sample

  • Peaks of 28S and 18S
  • Flat in fast region (indicate no degradation of RNA into fragmented RNA)

Centrosomes repeat may affect offspring chance

Posted on 2018-03-14

McNally, F. Competing chromosomes explain junk DNA. Science 358, 594–595 (2017).

Observation

One copy of a chromosome is inherited with >50% frequency in, for example, egg-polar body formation

Centromeric DNA

Contain >1000 short (100-300bp) sequences that evolve rapidly in copy number and sequence
Most abundant ncDNA in human genome

Hypothesis of these sequence

  • functional
  • selfish and promote their own inheritance without benefiting host organism

Example in mouse

Standard lab mouse (CF-1): 20 telocentric
Wild mouse (RB(6.16)): 10 metacentric

Stronger centromere

  • Tend to have 6-fold centromeric repeat sequence
  • Increase kinetochore protein level
  • Prefer to retain in egg

In Rb(6.16) telocentrics is stronger
In CF-1 fusion is stronger
Maybe it’s due to the relative centromere strength of metacentric fusion vs homologous telocentric

Possible mechanism


Strong centromere -> more kinetochore -> less stable attachment to tyrosinated microtubules -> break more often -> “flip” more often -> become stable -> tend to retain in egg
